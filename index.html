Chord Progression


Chord progression is what is used to describe the movement of music. Within virtually every song, you will find repeating tone patterns, or chords. A chord is typically a group of three or more notes played at or near the same time. We divide chords into seven groups, designating each by a roman numeral. After the seventh chord, the chords repeat, starting at the first, but simply played an octave higher. Each chord found this scale has a "base note" that corresponds with a note in a typical scale (represented by letters A through G). Here's an example of chord progression in a song.



Make sense?

In the weird way that music works, moving from certain chords to other chords sounds "good", while moving to wrong chords makes the music sound... dissonant. Grating. Luckily, because we listen to so much music, it's easy to instictively indentify what will sound good. As a musician, however, you are expected to be able to know what these patterns are and identify them.

Chord Examples

    • Minor: i
  2. D-F-A
    • Major: II
    • Minor: ii
  3. E-G-B
    • Major: III
    • Minor: iii
  4. F-A-C
    • Major: IV
    • Minor: iv
  5. G-B-D
    • Major: V
    • Minor: v
  6. A-C-E
    • Major: VI
    • Minor: vi
  7. B-D-F
    • Major: VII
    • Minor: vii
